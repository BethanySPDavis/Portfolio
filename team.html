  • What is it?

    • The assignment for the year 4 team project was to work more on our existing projects from the advanced game technology module. For the duration of this, we picked one project as a baseline to work on, added middleware, divided up the team into different roles, and worked remotely on making a networked multiplayer game with different levels. For this project, I took on the role of level design, and programmed various obstacles using tools the team had created. I also took an active role in the testing process, and all of the balancing that took place where working remotely had left discrepancies in how some parts of levels worked.



  • Why I chose my role

    • I asked to be in charge of level design initially because I’d wanted to further my skills in this area, having not had a huge opportunity to use it outside of a few levels I’d created in level design tools, such as Happy Wheels and Mario Maker 2. It was also an important role from a programming perspective, as I had to communicate specific things that I needed for my levels if they didn’t exist, and program them myself if the tools were available. As such, I was able to further my skills in multiple areas while in the context of a team.

  • How I made the levels

    • I initially planned out my levels in paint, drawing rough dimensions possible obstacles and how they’d fit into the course, and small explanations of what each one was doing, and the rationale behind them. Expecting them to change a lot more than they did, I then began placing the essentials, such as the walls and basic physics objects, then began programming the custom objects which needed to work in a specific way, such as boulders which needed to disappear after a certain amount of time to avoid cluttering the course, or objects which needed to be constantly rotating. Many of these were used extensively throughout both levels I created, and the level I wasn’t in charge of.

      While some of them were modified for the final project, and some elements of the levels proved too difficult, or didn’t necessarily work with our camera system or controls, I was present throughout the entire process, and was able to constantly modify them to keep up with the ongoing changes in the engine, and explain specific aspects as necessary.

  • Observations

    • This was my first time working remotely on a team project, and while I wasn’t sure exactly what to expect, the team had a great level of communication, were very easy to work with, and were available when I needed them for any assistance. I also very much enjoyed the project. From a creative aspect, working on the levels was a great way to explore ideas I had, and from a programming aspect, being able to easily put together complex objects using existing work the team had made as part of the engine was very satisfying.

  • How successful has it been?

    • Overall, the group received a mark of 66. While not what I’d hoped for, the commentary on the levels was broadly positive, stating that they worked well, although could have benefitted from a data-driven approach. I personally feel that I could have showed more of the team’s work off, however, I do think that what was there was well put together, and was overall a good showcase of the various different elements we had. Especially in being sure to design the levels from the ground up to work for multiplayer.

  • What would I do differently if I started over?

    • I would have followed the feedback about level design, stating that a data-driven approach may have been better. While a steeper learning curve, it would have been easier to finalise a structure that way, and could possibly have improved load times, while almost certainly making it easier to edit. I would also have looked into more ways off showing off what the team had made. While my levels showcased a lot, I am concerned that there was more I could have done.

  • How I've been able to use it

    • The project predictably provided a great deal of insight on how to develop within a team. I’d already had some experience in year 2 over the course of developing a mobile App, however, the year 4 project was more relevant to the kind of work I’m aiming to do in the future as part of a career. Additionally, the feedback about using a more data driven approach has carried over into my year 4 dissertation and Shuttle Engine, where I’ve been working on making sure that the engine and the levels which run on top are functionally separate entities. Finally, while this was tested in the extreme, this project was very useful on learning about remote collaboration. This skill may be less in demand after the end of lockdown, but I feel is a good demonstration of solid team work in the face of exceptional circumstances.
