  • What is it?

    • In the run up to the release of the Demon’s Souls remaster, a game I’d suspected was coming out since I first played the original in 2018, and had had confirmation that I was lucky enough to be able to get a copy on release day from September 2020, I had been on the fence about adding some speakers to my existing HiFi system to turn it into a full 7.1 surround sound in order to experience the game with the fullest change I could manage. I already had front left and right, the right amplifier set up to support the required 4 more speakers, and a centre and subwoofer. The issue was however, because it’s cobbled together from separates, mostly from the 80s to bring the cost down, and doesn’t have the required HDMI port in the amplifier to get the audio out that way, I had to come up with a way to change either the sound from the TV into separate RCA channels, or get the sound out of the HDMI signal from the console, prior to it reaching the TV, and extract it that way.   • Why I made it

    • Aside from having been a huge fan of the game and wanting to experience it in the best way possible, I also wanted the challenge of putting together a passable surround sound system on as low of a budget as possible, and as applicable as possible to as many of my sources as possible. The first challenge was deciding where to extract the sound. Not all TVs have the capability of outputting 7.1 from the optical port, and many don’t have any other ways of extracting surround sound. The TV I was using at the time for example could only go up to 5.1, so I could have compromised and used the optical from there. Alternatively, I’d been researching if it was possible to extract the PCM audio from the signal, repeat the signal with the video intact to pass it on to the TV (or in my case, the 1 in 2 out), and then separate the audio manually into 8 separate RCA channels for use with my older amplifiers. Luckily, I came across a device which was fairly affordable and probably due to the price range, thankfully didn’t have any additional effects on the audio, which did exactly that. Ultimately, what I’ve ended up with is a very competent system, which is extremely enjoyable to use, has very few issues (if you leave the TV unmuted from the built in speakers, there’s an echo for example, because the sound reaches the speakers before the TV can process it and send it to its own built in ones), and works on all of my devices with no formatting issues. Usually I wouldn’t put a more fanciful and extravagant project like this on this kind of document, but it was another interesting challenge to overcome creatively, similar to the mixer.
