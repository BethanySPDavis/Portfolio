  • What is it?

    • The goal of my project and dissertation for year 4 is to create a state machine based AI which uses simulated emotion in its decision making process. Similarly to my year 3 project and dissertation, it’s a project I’m approaching with the intention of using as a black box of sorts with other existing projects. It’s being programmed in C# for use with Shuttle Engine, and the current plan is to use two separate state machines (one for emotion, one for action) in a small simulated medieval village to test how the AI will process different systems, represented as jobs you might expect to find in a medieval village of questionable accuracy, such as fishing, soldiery, farming, trading, and smithing and other forms of crafts.

  • Why I'm making it

    • I picked this as a project because I wanted to get even more familiar with AI after my previous work on the GIF player, the Games Development Project, and previous coursework, and because I was inspired from having watched Star Trek: The Next Generation. Data's continual comments about not being able to feel emotion provoked me to start thinking about how you could make a robot feel emotions. Additionally, looking at episodes such as The Quality of Life, wherein Data believes small robots are exhibiting signs of self-preservation, it made me consider what benefit emotions would have to a robot. Data himself seems to frequently bring up the potential benefits to not feeling them for the purposes of his job, while also bringing up the negatives. In a game context however, this would be invaluable, as more human AI is something each generation of games has tried to achieve in numerous different ways. F.E.A.R. for example, a 15 year old game at time of writing is still notable and praised for its use of goal-oriented action planning AI.
      While many studios keep their techniques under wraps, the last major release to have any real clamour around its use of AI was Hello Games’ Hello Neighbor. While the field of AI is highly advanced in an academic sense, games seem to lag behind, not because they aren’t sufficiently advanced, purely because a game has a lot to juggle in keeping up with expected performance, and the more advanced the AI, the more expensive it is to run, which means the less likely a studio is to use it over existing technology. My solution was to create a light weight, simple AI, such as a state machine, then model it to respond to certain emotions, which could be updated from in game conditions, rather than creating any particularly elaborate way for the AI to feel. The desired goal would be the effects.

  • How it will work

    • The current plan I have in place is to use multiple state machines. As they’re fairly inexpensive to run, and the game tracks all of the stats which are used for it anyway, it shouldn’t be a huge performance drain to update several at once per character. At one level would be the animations of the character, the next level would be which action they’re taking, and at the very top would be a state machine determining what emotion the character should be feeling based on statistics it’s currently tracking, which in turn affect probability of transitioning between states, which then affect what the character does. The desired effect, is that for example, a soldier which is in a flock of 1, facing off against the player will be more defensive than a soldier in a flock of 10. Similarly, a smith who has had a piece of work paid for at a good price may be said to feel happy, and might output a higher quality of work at a faster rate. This will be measured against a corresponding control AI designed to do the same things without the emotions. My theory is that while subtle, the effects of the emotions should make the village more engaging over time (tricky to measure, although I will be able to look at printouts of probabilities of transitioning between various states, and barks from the characters as to how they’re feeling). The other desired outcome of course, is that there won’t be a great deal of performance change between the two different AI.

  • How successful has it been so far?

    • While the project is still progressing, the research I’ve managed to find so far has been encouraging, and the accompanying project in Shuttle Engine is at a point where everything I need for this project can just be added in without making any changes. Additionally, the planning stage is pretty much finished, and given that I’ve learned from my year 3 dissertation being badly planned if successful, it should help the remaining body of work take place significantly faster, and much more easily. It’s also a project I’m not having to create from scratch at a level of the principle, and has an existent body of work surrounding it, if fairly small.

  • What would I do differently if I started over?

    • While I can’t look back on the project just yet, I know I’d already rather have done it in C++. It goes back to the circular logic I used to justify making Shuttle Engine in C#, but ultimately, a lot of the code for this should be easily portable, so I’m not as concerned. Also, I might have made an earlier start, but there was somewhat of an overlap with the group project, and the planning stage went a lot better than I’d come to expect from my year 3 project and dissertation, so I’m not too concerned about that either.

  • How it will be useful to me in the future

    • As well as being a fairly deep dive into the world of AI, it’ll also be a welcome addition to my growing collection of black-box style projects I can simply plug into Shuttle Engine. So I hope that the knowledge I gain from working with AI at this level, in a state which is well researched, and still used extensively in commercial projects will be useful. I also hope it’s something I can reuse in future personal projects, as having an AI influenced by emotions in a project- even if they are modelled manually, completely simulated, and possibly difficult to set up properly- is no small claim to make.
