  • What is it?

    • The advanced game technology assignment was to create a short game based on the premise of Fall Guys using an engine provided to us for the project which used a simple physics engine, and a modified version of openGL. The features we were told to concentrate on involved getting elements of the physics engine to work, such as specific types of collision detection, creating a functional level with sensible mechanics of our own choosing, obstacles using techniques we were advised on, such as constraints, and the inclusion of AI players to compete against.

  • How it works

    • For my project, I chose to focus on making a short level work as well as it could in a self-contained way, with a simple text menu for choosing different options, an assessment of win states and scores, and the ability to close the game. I also made sure to include various features I deemed as essential, and then worked from there to add complexity, for example kill planes for when players inevitably fell off the platform, basic obstacles to add some difficulty to it, cubes which would add a score to the player which the AI enemies could also pick up, a finish line to trigger the end of the game, and basic A* pathfinding for the AI which attempted to avoid the obstacles where possible.


  • Observations

    • The game loop itself was something that worked quite well, and I was pleased with this, as it was very much a continuation of existing projects wherein I’d created specific game loops. While this is a simple end of the project, I take a lot of pride in my efforts with it, and made sure to implement it well. Additionally, the project was a good exercise in managing feature creep, as I had a hard deadline, and I’d ordered parts of the project I was working on so the highest priority elements came first, and when I had less time, I could focus on specific areas I thought I’d do well in.

  • How successful has it been?

    • Unfortunately I didn’t have time to complete certain features, such as object oriented bounding boxes, or capsules, which would have allowed me to add more detail to my game in the form of slopes to increase player speeds, hills to make being knocked down more of a penalty, or better collision detection by making the players capsules. I did achieve a mark of 73 overall, however I feel there was definitely more I could add in terms of making the game play much better. Additionally, there were certainly balance issues I’d like to have gone back and fixed. Not to mention some of the obstacles being unnecessarily frustrating rather than engaging. I’m happy with the mark however, and I was somewhat pleased with the results, even if the game itself isn’t something I’d choose to play in its present state.

  • What would I do differently if I started over?

    • I’d possibly have prioritised different elements of the game, although this would have resulted in more of a reshuffling of the final product where something else was removed to make space for it, so this isn’t something I’ve considered in great detail. The one thing I would definitely do which wouldn’t have massively affected time however, is planning the level. This is something I would later do on the team project, and has proved to be of great assistance, not only in making the level easier to put into the program, but also in terms of making a more complex and interesting level, as it’s far easier to see the bigger picture and flow of it all in a plan, rather than as I’m developing it.

  • How I've been able to use it

    • This was a particularly good project for working further on AI, as I had an opportunity to design a simple NPC AI which would follow its own goals based on in game elements, such as the position of the player (if there was an AI closer to the goal, the other AI would focus on getting to the end. If the player was closer to the goal, the AI would gang up on the player and try to get in their way). This has been somewhat influential in considering how I’ll do some of the work in my year 4 project and dissertation. It’s also helped solidify some simple concepts in programming for games which I had some trouble with- although possibly due to engine problems- in year 3 on the game simulations module.
